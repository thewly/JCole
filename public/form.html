Salish Nation

Since learning about the clam gardens in the Puget Sound area near Seattle, I’ve been intrigued by the people who developed them. These gardens are carbon-dated upwards of 5000 years old, according to archeological findings, but modern scientific research confirms that their methods did (and do), in fact, produce better quality clams and a higher yield, thus improving food security:
“…Overall, clam gardens contained 4 times as many butter clams and over twice as many littleneck clams relative to non-walled beaches. As predicted, this relationship varied as a function of intertidal height, whereby clam density and biomass tended to be greater in clam gardens compared to non-walled beaches at relatively higher intertidal heights. Transplanted juvenile L. staminea grew 1.7 times faster and smaller size classes were more likely to survive in clam gardens than non-walled beaches, specifically at the top and bottom of beaches. Consequently, we provide strong evidence that ancient clam gardens likely increased clam productivity by altering the slope of soft-sediment beaches, expanding optimal intertidal clam habitat, thereby enhancing growing conditions for clams. These results reveal how ancient shellfish aquaculture practices may have supported food security strategies in the past and provide insight into tools for the conservation, management, and governance of intertidal seascapes today. (Groesbeck AS, Rowell K, Lepofsky D, Salomon AK (2014) Ancient Clam Gardens Increased Shellfish Production: Adaptive Strategies from the Past Can Inform Food Security Today.” https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0091235)
Who were these ingenious clam-farmers? And what do we know about their descendants there today? “Salish” encompasses many tribes of the Pacific Northwest, spreading from British Columbia down through the Puget Sound area of the United States’ western shoreline and expanding inland (https://native-americans.com/salish-tribes/)

Vast and Diverse

There were many diverse groups associated with the Salish (or S’Elish) and related tribes, some of which are: Chemakum (one of the many extinct), Tlingit, Nisga’a (from the extinct Tsetsaut nation, now speaking Athabaskan), Haida, Tsimshian, Gitxsan, Haisla, Heiltsuk, Nuxalk, Kwakwaka’wakw (17 tribes left, 250 people speaking Kwak’wala), Nuu-chah-nulth, Southern Wakashan, Makah, Coast Salish, Squamish, Chimakum (mostly extinct from being defeated by Chief Seattle), Quileute, Willapa (speak Athabaskan) Chinook, Nehalem (Tillamook), and Da’naxda’xw (only 225 members left as of 2018). ( http://www.ncsl.org/research/state-tribal-institute/list-of-federal-and-state-recognized-tribes.aspx)

Ancient Practice, Modern Science

This family of tribes under the heading of Salish is not one of the most written-about Indigenous peoples. Apparently it doesn’t enjoy the seeming near-constant study that, for example, the Diné do (remembering the joke about every Navajo family including a father, mother, children, and an archeologist). Suitable reference material through my libraries and on the Internet was limited, and I almost changed direction more than once. However, this ancient society (that had figured out and utilized a system that modern science verifies helps the food supply and ecosystem) remain my chosen subject.

Language Matters

Many of these nations' languages are, sadly, extinct. Among the remaining few, several have only a handful of Native speakers, most elderly in age. It is estimated that by 2050 most of the remaining languages will be extinct, due to most of the younger generations not learning and using the tongue of their grandmothers. The tragedy of dying languages lies in the fact that each language encapsulates its own culture, history, place names, and sacred references. So when the language dies, a huge part of the culture dies with it- even though the DNA of the remaining peoples may continue on.

Devastation and Loss

A smallpox epidemic in the late 1700’s (just prior to European explorers coming from the eastern part of the continent) wiped out an estimated 70% of the Salish peoples. The smallpox epidemic is speculated to have come from contact with peoples from the south. Here’s one account of their first encounter with the Salish, whom they had supposed would be very numerous, but they saw only a few:
‘On May 21, 1792, Peter Puget discovered further signs of this disease on the Puget Sound residents. While Lieutenant Puget explored the southern reaches of the sound soon to receive his name, he met some Indians in a canoe. He stated that “Two of the three in the Canoe had lost the Right Eye & were much pitted with the Small Pox, which Disorder in all probability is the Cause of that Defect…” (Peter Puget, PNW Quarterly, 198). On August 18, 1792, while near the Queen Charlotte Islands, Peter Puget gave a summary description of the Indians of Puget Sound and the Strait of Georgia: “[T]he Small pox most have had, and most terribly pitted they are; indeed many have lost their Eyes, & no Doubt it has raged with uncommon Inacteracy among them.” (Robert Boyd, The Coming of the Spirit of Pestilence (Vancouver, BC: UBC Press, 1999), 22-24, 27-30, 34, 36-37, 55, 204-205, 273, 293-295)’

Recording History

A missionary/researcher from England, Charles Hill-Tout, had loved time spent in the Vancouver area and eventually returned there with his family to settle among the Indians he was fascinated with. He had lived among them previously and recorded “a young chief of the Chehalis Indians and his wife, who had been recently married, gave up their bedroom to me and also their bedding while they slept upon the floor of the kitchen for two weeks.” (Hill-Tout, Charles: The Salish People, The Local Contribution of Charles Hill-Tout Volume ii: The Squamish and the Lillooet, Edited with an introduction by Ralph Maud, pg 17).
During his missionary time, Hill-Tout met Chief Mischelle and found him to be a great talker, so he wrote down all the legends, stories, and history that Mischelle had to tell. This turned out to be extremely fortuitous, since this chief took ill about a year later. In about 1899, Chief Mischelle “was looking forward to the time when he would be so far recovered as to be able to take a sweat-bath”, but died soon after. Fortunately the stories recorded by Hill-Tout were published. (“Sqaktkquacit, or the Benign-faced, the Cannes of the Ntlakapamuq, British Columbia”).
The words of the Chief, as recorded by Hill-Tout, give us many of the Salish tales in a respectful, if not perfect, form. Hill-Tout’s many asides describing the Chief reveal Hill-Tout’s utter adoration of this individual and the culture he passed on through his stories. For example, in a section where the Squamish dead were described, he wrote, ”Here the old man’s voice was hushed to a plaintive wail, and the faces of his audience were an eloquent index of the tragic interest of this story of their ancestors’ misfortunes” (Hill-Tout, vol ii pg 17) .
One of the tales told by Chief Mishelle describes a personified woodpecker (the red ‘cap’ he wore having been given previously as a reward for noble behavior) who is married to two wives: a black bear and a grizzly. Each bore him three sons, but eventually jealousy brings one wife to plot against the other and leads to both wives’ deaths, the woodpecker’s demise, and the deaths of half his children- one being accidentally eaten by his own mother! The Chief, noted Hill-Tout, loved to relate these many stories from his culture, and would talk literally until the audience was falling asleep (Hill-Tout vol. ii).

Modern Salish Experience

So with all that the Salish have been through, are there many left? Yes, descendants of this ancient nation are among us as well as in the Pacific Northwest of the U.S. and the southern part of Canada and parts of B.C. and Alaska. In an interview, Nicholas Peterson is interviewed by Corey Smallcanyon about his Salish ties:
CS: On your mother’s side have you done any family history?
NP: Very limited. There’s a small circle of language that’s categorized as Athelbaskin, and our language isn’t Athelbaskin language. It’s just interior coastal natives. It is a relatively smaller band. It has a total of about twelve hundred members. The language is nearly dead. There’s only a handful of people that can still speak it. There are books and things written and they teach it in the band school, but they only have a dozen kids that come to school.
The band seems to be quite small. It is a valley that we live in. The valley’s not that big, but we live in what we call Lower Nicola Valley. The upper part of the valley is called Upper Nicola Valley. There is the Lower Nicola Indian band which I’m a part of and then the upper Nicola Indian band which is literally ten miles away. They speak a totally different language and the dialect varies from that language. It’s more Okenagen. Okenagen-type people were all over throughout even down to Montana in the Okenagen Valley, but ten miles away they speak a different language.
On the other side of the mountain twenty or thirty miles away, it is more the Salish people. We’re considered Salish Coastal Natives, but the Salish don’t speak an Athelbaskin either. There are only small patches in the Nicola Valley. I don’t know where it stems from or where they claim to come from of how our heritage is all tied together.
CS: With your kids being part Navajo, Salish, and white, when it comes time for registering them, what tribe do you go with?
NP: We never looked into the Navajo tribe. I don’t know how that all works. Vileena never really informed me. I know in Canada they give us a tribal card and a band number. I registered Louise and Anslo both in Canada in my tribe. I believe their kids will not qualify, though. I don’t know what the exact percentage is, but for their kids won’t qualify for membership unless they marry another band member or even a Canadian Native.
With the imaginary border between Canada and the U.S., if Vileena were to move to Canada she wouldn’t be recognized as a First Nation or a Native because she’s Navajo from the U.S. But luckily for me the U.S. usually recognizes a Canadian Native. I don’t know why that. From my experience, maybe there are loopholes, but when we were contemplating moving up there or having Vileena come up for different things that she wasn’t able to really get anything or to show that she was a Native America or First Nations person. CS: As far as your kis…what have you tried to teach them as far as culturally from …your cultural perspective or maybe Vileena’s because she’s Navajo?
NP: I am teaching them values and principles of obedience, honesty and integrity. As far as cultural things, one of the main reasons why we go to Canada every summer is to go berry picking and to go salmon fishing. That’s a very big part of my life that I grew up with and I really enjoy. It’s deep in our culture. Every year there’s a big salmon fish, and we get enough salmon to last us all year. …One fish tradition is we use a dipnet and gillnets. We don’t fish with a pole or bait, so I want to implement that. We berry pick. I want to find local fruits and foods. Of course, Native Americans did that for a sense of survival. It is sort of mountain men… and having a sense of being able to teach a respect for Mother Nature and to realize that she’s the one that provides for us. I try to take my kids camping and to help them recognize that and be respectful. (Nicholas Peterson Interviewed by Corey Smallcanyon, July 23, 2008 for the Charles Redd Center for Western Studies, Redd Center Oral History Project, Brigham Young Univ.)

Culture Reborn

A recent article about today’s Salish returning to ancient practices gives hope for the Nation’s future: “Clam gardens have been around on the British Columbia, Alaskan, and Washington state coasts for over 3000 years," Augustine says. After colonization, those gardens fell into disuse. Now, Augustine is running the first project since then to restore the gardens in British Columbia. The Swinomish's would be the first revival of this tradition in the U.S. At the annual Swinomish clam bake, more than a hundred tribal members gathered at long tables at a beach on the reservation, eating all kinds of shellfish. "There's the old Indian saying in this part of the world that when the tide is out the table is set," Swinomish elder Larry Campbell says, "because the clams are in our tideland." The goal of the clam garden is to keep the table set. The Swinomish Tribe hopes that its new garden will be built as soon as next summer, in time for the next clam bake.” (https://www.npr.org/2019/10/08/767896285/pacific-northwest-tribes-face-climate-change-with-agricultural-ancient-practice)
So whether we are appreciating the ingenious seafood harvesting (a renewal of ancient practices), reading the long history and cultural variety of the Salish, or appreciating part of their recorded oral history, we can see that the Salish were anciently a large and varied collection of peoples which still today are making their voices heard all across the western U.S, Canada, and B. C.
